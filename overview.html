Overview

This section serves as a very short introduction to the scanning tunneling microscope; its working principles, possibilities and constraints.

Introduction

The scanning tunneling microscope was invented by Gerd Binning and Heinrich Rohrer in 1981 in IBM, for which they received the Nobel Prize in physics for in 1986. The tool is a powerful microscopic technique capable of achieving sub-nanometer resolution of surfaces and is today routinely used by scientists to visualize and image surface topographies based on the local density of states concept [1]. By using a probe with an atomically sharp tip and an applied bias, a highly sensitive tunneling current can be conducted into the sample. Consequently, scanning the surface and measuring the changes in tunneling current results in an image representing the surfaces electronic environment [2]. This enables researchers to obtain a better understanding about their subject on a molecular level. One advantage over other characterization methods is its great versatility as it can operate in air, vacuum and liquids under non ambient conditions. However, one major disadvantage is the use of highly specialized equipment that is fragile and expensive. The commercial prices of quality microscopes can range from $30 000 to $150 000 and are thus inaccessible to nonprofessional users. However, via the introduction of cheap tunnelling microscopes, the market is extended to applicants with limited budgets such as scientific communities in development countries, students in engineering and science educations as well as hobbyists.

Figure 1 illustrates the main components we used in the scanning tunneling microscope. An arduino microcontroller is used to control a piezoelectic crystal and a stepper motor. The crystal is used to actuate an atomically sharp probe over the sample specimen and the stepper motor is connected to a micrometer screw gauge, allowing fine and course adjustments. We have connected four digital to analog converters to the arduino to allow the crystal to be controlled with high precision. By approaching the sample into a nanometer proximity to the probe and applying a bias, a tunneling current can be established. Consequently, the probe can be scanned over the surface, yielding an image corresponding to the surfaces local electronic environment. High resolutions can be obtained via this technique due to the tunneling currents exponential dependence with respect to the probe to surface distance. In practice however, this puts high demands on the equipment used. The setup has to be isolated from ambient vibrations and very small probe adjustments in the order of Ångström has to be made in order to fully take advantage of the tunneling phenomena.
