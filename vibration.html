Vibration isolation

In scanning tunneling microscopy the choice of material is highly important. Floor vibrations with amplitudes up to 100 nanometers must be suppressed in order to allow an atomic resolution of 0.1 nanometers. This puts severe limitations on the materials that can be used as the resonant frequency of the microscope is one of the factors that prevents imaging under ambient conditions.

Theory

As with regular photography, it is important to ensure that the subject and the camera are stationary during imaging. To remove the influence of ambient vibrations, you must tailor the resonance frequencies to remove as many frequencies as possible. The tip itself will always be a thin metal wire, and as such, will have a high resonance frequency. Low frequency vibrations will have little effect in disturbing the imaging, so long as the sample doesn’t move. High frequencies, however, will need to be prevented from reaching the tip and sample. By making a base with a low resonance frequency, this can be achieved. An explanation of how and why this works is easiest to get by studying transfer functions. The transfer function shows the response of a system to various frequencies. A system with high resonance frequency reduces the amplitude of low frequency vibrations, see figure 1, whereas a low resonance frequency dampens high frequencies, see figure 2. By putting together two systems with different resonance frequencies, their transfer functions are multiplied. A HF system combined with a LF system, with their respective resonance frequencies far from each other, will create a compound system which dampens almost all frequencies.

Implementation

The choice of aluminium as material for the body worked in favour of the vibration dampening, as it has a high resonance frequency. The base, which is supposed to remove high frequencies, is in our design split into three parts. First, towards the table, we have four feet made out of tennis balls cut open. On top of these rested a solid slab of concrete, of about 3 kg. Lastly, between the slab and the microscope body, we placed a silicone mat. We made our own mat from silicone glue squeezed into a cardboard box. After hardening, it became rather rigid, and it may be better to buy an already made mat. A soft mouse mat should also be a good choice. This combination ensured that most of the disturbing vibrations were filtered out, which means that a high resolution image is at least possible
